02 — Weekly Planner

The planner solves a real problem — with structural fixes needed

Every student who saw the planner responded positively. But day-level rigidity, fixed exam dates, and a 90-minute cap limit real-world usefulness.

💡

Key finding: Students consistently struggle to build their own revision schedules. Two students (Alina & Maya) had independently tried and failed to build their own plan 1–2 weeks before the interview: “We both tried it… we wrote all the topics down and everything. But we just haven’t stuck to it at all… Being a bit too ambitious.” The planner directly addresses this — the most commonly cited pain point in the study.

03 — Exam Questions

Quality is inconsistent — excellent at A-level, problematic at GCSE

Students value exam questions but trust is undermined by three issues: questions don’t match real exam wording, marking rejects correct answers, and questions misalign with topics just studied.

🔄

Counterpoint: A-level students were strongly positive about AI marking, especially for open-ended subjects like Psychology. And for subjects with few published past papers (e.g. computer science), Seneca’s question bank fills a genuine gap. The quality issue is not uniform — it is inconsistent across subjects and year groups.

⚠️

Questions feel unrealistic

GCSE students find question wording and format doesn’t match what they see in real exams. The gap erodes trust in Seneca as exam preparation.

“Test questions, I just found, like, it’s a bit unrealistic. They don’t really give common questions that you’d see in a test.”
Yahya · Y10
🔗

Topic misalignment

Exam questions sometimes don’t match the topic just studied, breaking the learn-then-test loop that students rely on.

“I really, really enjoy Seneca, and I don’t actually wanna go onto another website to find more exam questions. I just think if they were more specific.”
Izzy · Y11
❌

Marking too strict

Correct-in-substance answers are rejected when they don’t match exact mark scheme phrasing. Most acute in business and English.

“I get an answer right, but the mark scheme is on a different thing. So it’s kind of hard to get the mark.”
Zion · Y10
“
It’s better than just marking ourselves. Sometimes mark schemes are hard to understand. So especially for psychology, with AI marking it, it’s much more useful.

“
For computer science, there’s only about three papers since the new specification, so having a wide range of targeted new exam questions from Seneca is really helpful.
AaryanY11
“

04 — Themes

Patterns across all interviews

Six cross-cutting themes emerged consistently across the sample, from revision habits to feature requests and motivation.

🎯

Focus & concentration is the universal challenge

Every student aged Y10+ identified staying focused as their primary difficulty. The planner helps by removing decision fatigue but doesn’t address within-session attention.

“It’s just like staying focused. Yeah. It’s just basically just staying focused.”
Yahya · Y10
👪

Parent accountability is valued, not resented

Three students mentioned parent involvement positively. They want to show parents what they’ve done — the planner gives them something concrete to point to.

“I have my mum on Seneca so she can see how long I do it for, and it’d be quite nice just to prove — hello, this is what I’ve done.”
Izzy · Y11
🏆

Social features emerging organically

Students are self-organising peer competition around XP. But they also self-regulate — Zion flagged leaderboards could be pressuring and gameable. Grade-progress feedback preferred over rankings.

“You could see you’ve moved up one more grade that you normally are on — because that’s self affirmative.”
Zion · Y10
👁

Discoverability failures block adoption silently

Motivated students can’t find features. Izzy couldn’t find the planner on any device. Neither Alina nor Maya noticed recommendations. Multiple students didn’t know about the mobile app.

“I thought it was just that you have to search up to get [to Seneca] — I actually didn’t know it was an app.”
Izzy · Y11
🔔

Notifications & reminders are a missing activation layer

Students who value the planner still forget to open it. Three students explicitly requested push notifications or email reminders at their scheduled study time.

“It’d be quite nice to have a reminder button. Some days I’ll be like, oh shoot, I need to do my Seneca.”
Izzy · Y11
🤖

AI tools are already competing for scheduling

Zion uses Microsoft Copilot for revision scheduling. Students aren’t waiting for Seneca — they’re building solutions with whatever tools are available.

“It’s similar [to Copilot], but… so far it’s really good.”
Zion · Y10
05 — Recommendations

Prioritised actions for the planner

Synthesised from frequency, salience, and cross-student convergence. Ranked by impact on planner adoption and retention.

P0 — Do Now
1
High Priority

Allow custom exam/mock date input

The default May endpoint is wrong for Y10 mock students (April), A-level students with staggered exams, and students who want longer plans. A student whose mocks are in April will immediately see the plan as irrelevant.

“I would probably set the end date to be, like, when I start my mocks. Like, I start my mocks in April 2026.”Isabella · Y10
“An end point would actually be nice. It kinda, like, mocks where your test is.”Yahya · Y10
“We could make it a bit more personal to when our exams actually start.”Maya · Y13
“If I made it longer, it’s gonna help me be consistent over week… week and [week].”Zion · Y10
2
High Priority

Add daily subject swap/override

Let students choose which subject to study on a given day without disrupting the weekly plan. The most requested structural feature — addresses tutor conflicts, school tests, and personal preferences.

“I would prefer to choose what subjects I do on each day. Because I have a tutor for English.”Aaryan · Y11
“Maybe choosing which one you can do on which day depending on, like, whether you have, like, a test in school the next day.”Isabella · Y10
P1 — Do Next
3
Medium Priority

Add push notifications / email reminders

Three students requested this explicitly. The gap between intention and action is attentional, not motivational. Also: actively promote the mobile app — multiple students were unaware it exists.

“It’d be quite nice to have a reminder button. Some days I’ll be like, oh shoot, I need to do my Seneca.”Izzy · Y11
“I probably [would] put email notifications, like, to remind students.”Yahya · Y10
4
Medium Priority

Raise or remove the 90-minute daily cap

Allow students to set higher daily targets for intensive revision periods. A 90-minute cap means the planner covers only a fraction of serious revisers’ days during half term, Easter, and study leave.

“Ninety minutes is good for some people, but some people allocate three, four, even five hours to revision.”Aaryan · Y11
5
Medium Priority

Audit exam question topic mapping

Exam questions are sometimes mapped at too broad a topic level, breaking the learn-then-test loop. Start with biology where specific examples were reported.

“I then started asking me about plants, and I was like, what? I was just a bit confused.”Izzy · Y11
“I really, really enjoy Seneca, and I don’t actually wanna go onto another website to find more exam questions.”Izzy · Y11
P2 — Consider
6
Explore

End-of-week review and progress feedback

Students want to know how they did each week and what to adjust. Close the “how did I do?” gap that the planner currently leaves open.

“At the end of one week, why don’t you add mini papers — where you can do mini mocks.”Zion · Y10
“Just more targeted exam questions. So if I’ve done badly on something, I can get more practice on that.”Maya · Y13

Methodology: 7 semi-structured interviews with 8 students conducted 18–19 February 2026. Transcripts captured via Granola (auto-transcription).
