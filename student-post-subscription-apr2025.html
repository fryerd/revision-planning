Seneca · Student Post-Subscription Survey · Apr 2025 – Apr 2026

The year Cram mode reshaped student conversion

A year of post-subscription feedback from 846 students who upgraded to Seneca Premium between April 2025 and April 2026. This report traces how a single commercial decision — placing Cram mode behind a paywall — reshaped the conversion conversation across three seasonal periods, and what it reveals about where Seneca's B2C growth lever really sits.

846 student responses 12 months of continuous fielding 8-question in-product survey 3 periods · exam, autumn, spring

The one-minute takeaway

Seneca's B2C conversion in 2025/26 is defined by three things: a paywall mechanic that worked (Cram mode gating converted the largest exam-season cohort Seneca has collected), a feature story stabilising (Mini-mocks is the lead feature, Exam Questions and Cram mode complete the top three, Amelia sits as the premium tier), and a commercial gap that was finally closed — a monthly subscription shipped in February 2026 after a year of testing to 10% of users, which reshapes the "let me pay monthly" hesitation that had appeared in every post-subscription survey since October 2022. Competitive pressure is real but manageable — ChatGPT as an Amelia substitute is the one strategic signal to act on quickly.

The Cram Mode story — in three acts

In April 2025 Seneca placed Cram mode — previously free and unlimited — behind a paywall. It was the most aggressive feature gating since Mini-mocks in 2023. A subsequent A/B test softened it to a 20-question partial-free gate. This year's survey captures the full emotional arc in real time.

Act 1 · Exam 2025

The gating lands

337 responses — 22% more than exam season 2024 on the same instrument. The conversion lift is real.

Unlimited Cram mode enters the multi-select at 60% feature appeal — the highest debut share of any new feature option across the post-subscription surveys.

Dozens of students name Cram mode as the only reason they upgraded. The anger is loud and concentrated in April–May.

Act 2 · Autumn 2025

The heat cools

Cram-mode mentions drop in volume and soften in tone. The motivation voice stops sounding coerced.

New students coming to Seneca don't know Cram used to be free — they just know it's part of Premium.

Feature appeal settles at 54% — still top-three, but no longer a lightning rod.

Act 3 · Spring 2026

Normalised

Cram mode is integrated into the everyday motivation vocabulary. "Quizzing and cram mode" is a typical motivation answer.

By now, Mini-mocks has resurged to 80% feature appeal — the product story has moved on.

The commercial benefit persists; the emotional cost had a half-life of roughly three months.

The read for the roadmap

The gating worked commercially — P1 delivered the largest exam-season sample Seneca has collected in a post-subscription survey. But the emotional cost was real and concentrated. The subsequent softening to a 20-question partial-free gate appears to be the right response. Any future gating decision should bank both outcomes into its expectations: a conversion lift and a spike of adversarial voice that needs communications-level care. By Spring 2026 new students don't remember the change at all — the anger half-life is roughly three months.

Mini-mocks: 80% in Spring '26

After softening to 73–76% across the autumn 2024 survey and P1/P2 of this one, Mini-mocks recovered to 80% in P3 Spring 2026 — near its historical peak. Whatever was shipped on the feature in the past year has landed.

Hardest questions growing quietly

36% (P1) → 38% (P2) → 42% (P3). One of the few features gaining ground. Worth naming and investing in as a distinct value proposition.

Wrong Answer Mode softening

Stuck at 38% across all three periods — down from 44% in the previous year's survey. As Cram mode and Mini-mocks dominate the revision loop, Wrong Answers is losing relative appeal.

Why they actually upgrade

Behind the package and feature choices sits the emotional driver. Exam anxiety is still the primary one — but the shape of the conversation has become more specific, more peer-influenced, and more regret-aware.

The monthly subscription ask

Students asking for a monthly option has appeared in every post-subscription survey since October 2022. It became sharper during P1 and P2 of this survey, amplified by the Cram gating — students wanted Cram for one specific month but were forced into a four-month commitment. A monthly package shipped in February 2026 after a year of testing with 10% of users — the pre-Feb-2026 quotes below are what made the case.

First appeared in the autumn 2024 post-subscription survey (Sep 2024 – Apr 2025) and has continued into this one. Students now explicitly reference peer and review-site negativity in their pre-purchase thinking. None of the surveys before autumn 2024 contained this signal at all.

Pricing transparency is leaking trust

A pricing-comms problem has surfaced: students see one price in marketing or on the initial screen and a higher one at checkout. The word "misleading" is used by both parents and students. This is now especially relevant with the monthly option live, since monthly-vs-seasonal price differences are a fresh source of confusion.

Opportunities and threats for the B2C business

The post-purchase voice, read across a full year, is not just diagnostic — it points at concrete levers and competitive pressure points.

Opportunities

Opportunity · Commercial NB: shipped Feb 2026

Monthly subscription

The single most-repeated hesitation in every post-subscription survey since October 2022 — sharpened further by the Cram gating during P1/P2 of this survey, where students wanted Cram for one specific month but faced a four-month commitment. A monthly package shipped in February 2026 after a year of testing with 10% of users. The watching-brief now is monthly-vs-seasonal pricing perception, not availability.

Opportunity · Product

Mini-mocks has room to extend its lead

Back to 80% feature appeal in Spring 2026 after softening. The strongest single-feature conversion asset. More content depth, more subjects, better feedback loops — all compound on the most valued thing students buy.

Opportunity · Product

Close named course coverage gaps

"There isn't a course for OCR psychology GCSE — that was the only thing I was debating" (Y11, March 2025). One articulated missing course converted to a multi-week delay. The clearest coverage-gap-as-lost-revenue quote captured in the post-subscription surveys.

Opportunity · Positioning

Lean into the "cost of not buying"

Quotes like "I didn't get it at GCSE and now I'm not allowed to do A-Level Chemistry" and "I need an academic miracle" are marketing gold. The fear of under-investing is a stronger conversion lever than the excitement of feature access.

Opportunity · Feature

Invest in "Hardest questions"

Quietly growing feature appeal across every period (36% → 38% → 42%). One of the few rising signals in the dataset. Worth confirming what it maps to in-product and surfacing prominently.

Opportunity · Ops

Fix the pricing-comms confusion

Students and parents now use "misleading" to describe the price-teaser-at-marketing vs. higher-commitment-at-checkout flow. Especially relevant now the monthly option is live — monthly-vs-seasonal price differences are a fresh source of confusion that needs crystal-clear communication.

Threats

Threat · Competitive

ChatGPT is now named as an Amelia substitute

"The price is quite expensive especially the AI package for when you can just use a platform like ChatGPT for free." First explicit substitute-quote in the post-subscription surveys. Amelia's differentiation — curriculum awareness, progress integration, safety — needs sharper positioning before this pattern scales.

Threat · Competitive

The competitor set has broadened

In this and the previous year's survey, students named Uplearn, Savemyexams, BBC Bitesize, NotebookLM, GCSEpod and ChatGPT in their pre-purchase reasoning. Pre-purchase comparison is now standard — earlier post-subscription surveys contained near-zero competitor mentions.

Threat · Brand

Peer negativity is in the voice for the first time

"People say it's not worth it" and "bad reviews saying it was useless" did not appear in any post-subscription survey before autumn 2024. They appear now. External review defence (Trustpilot, App Store) is now a real marketing discipline.

Threat · Emotional

Aggressive gating creates a review-risk tail

The April 2025 Cram gating produced a small but vivid cohort saying "I will be leaving a bad review". The heat cooled within three months, but anyone searching for Seneca reviews during that window saw the cost of the decision. Any future gating needs a paired communications plan.
